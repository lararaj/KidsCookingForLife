I hope you all have been staying well and active this past week while some places have begun to open up. Are you enjoying becoming confident, healthy chefs? This week's topic, the Dairy Group, completes the food groupings that you need to include each day. Milk and dairy foods play a major role in children’s diets by providing many of the nutrients required for optimal growth and development.  There is only a short window of time to build your bones and keep them strong for the rest of your life.  Before you are 30 years old, you will have reached your 'peak bone mass", the strongest your bones will ever be.  Now is the time to build those bones, to keep them strong and reach your fullest potential.
 
Milk and dairy foods are a nutritious, accessible option that positively impacts overall dietary patterns, but many children who avoid this food group are undernourished.  This is particularly true in vulnerable communities, where the food and beverage choices people make are often driven by availability, accessibility and affordability.  In addition, some people get intestinal discomfort from lactose, the milk’s natural sugar (or disaccharide). However, people with lactose intolerance don’t have to miss the benefits of dairy products.   Many can even consume moderate amounts and I am happy to tell you about how to enjoy them with out discomfort and provide you with tips when you need alternatives.
 
Stay healthy until next time,

Nutrition Notes

Scientific research confirms that milk, yogurt and cheese offer a unique package of nutrients—calcium, vitamin D, potassium and more—that work together to provide multiple health benefits, and reduce the risk of developing chronic diseases such as type 2 diabetes and heart disease.
 
Until recently, most information about the mineral, calcium and vitamin D content in dairy products focused on teeth and bone building and preventing osteoporosis later in life.  Did you know that calcium also helps maintain a normal heartbeat, is involved in muscle contractions, helps the nervous system, and regulates blood pressure too? And, that with out vitamin D, we can’t absorb calcium very well and we also need it for our immune system?
 
Calcium influences so many vital functions. If you don’t give your body enough, the body will find its own source and will actually pull it out of the bones if it's needed somewhere else. Take it from me, I’ve experienced this first hand and early osteoporosis as a result. That's why meeting your daily allowance is crucial through out your life.
 
According to the NIH, and translated by the USDA, the amounts of calcium required for bone health and retention for kids are :
 
4–8 years, 2.5 cups/day or 1,000 mg
9–18 years, 3 cups/day or 1,300 mg

 
Eating these servings of calcium-rich foods daily helps ensure that the body will maintain a good supply of calcium. The wide variety of milk and dairy foods available provides many options to meet personal needs and tastes, including lactose-free milk and yogurt.
 
Dairy foods offer health attributes that are different from plant-based and other animal-source foods due to their complex mix of nutrients, bio-actives and other components. Dairy milk, for example, has the most balanced distribution of energy from carbohydrates, protein and fat, then any other single food.

 

Experts do not recommend calcium supplements for children, preferring dietary sources to meet daily recommendations. See the USDA website and the following list of food sources of calcium:
 
Yogurt, plain, low fat, 8 ounces 415mg
Orange juice, calcium fortified, 1 cup 349mg
Mozzarella, part skim, 1.5 ounces 333mg
Sardines, canned in oil, with bones, 3 ounces 325mg
Cheddar cheese, 1.5 ounces 307mg
Milk, nonfat, 1 cup** 299mg
Soymilk, calcium fortified, 1 cup 299mg
Tofu, firm, made with calcium sulfate, ½ cup*** 253mg
Salmon, pink, canned, solids with bone, 3 ounces 181mg
Cottage cheese, 1% milk fat, 1 cup 138mg
Breakfast cereals, fortified with 10% of the DV for calcium, 1 serving 130mg
Frozen yogurt, vanilla, soft serve, ½ cup 103mg
Turnip greens, fresh, boiled, ½ cup 99mg
Kale, fresh, cooked, 1 cup 94mg
Chia seeds, 1 tablespoon 76mg
 Bok Choy, raw, shredded, 1 cup 74mg
Tortilla, corn, one, 6” diameter 46mg
Broccoli, raw, ½ cup 21mg
 
FDA requires all food labels to list calcium content. Foods providing 20% or more of the DV are considered to be high sources of a nutrient.
** Calcium content varies slightly by fat content; the more fat in the food, the less calcium it contains.
*** Calcium content is for tofu processed with a calcium salt. Tofu processed with other salts does not provide significant amounts of calcium.
 
Lactose-intolerant individuals and vegans are at risk of calcium inadequacy. Use the MyPlate food group activity or data base to check on your regular intake of the foods in this group.  Just add up the amounts of calcium from all sources on an average day to be sure to get at least the minimum amount for age.
 
Most people with lactose intolerance can consume up to 12 grams of lactose, such as that present in 8 ounces of milk, with minimal or no symptoms, especially if consumed with other foods; larger amounts can frequently be consumed if spread over the day and eaten with other foods.  Other options to reduce symptoms include eating low-lactose dairy products such as aged cheeses (Cheddar and Swiss), yogurt, or lactose-reduced or lactose-free milk. To ensure you meet your calcium and Vit D goals, fortified foods added with them, such as certain tofus, orange juices, and cereals, plus nondairy vegetable sources such as kale, bok choy, broccoli, and collard greens can help.
 
Data shows that most Americans are not meeting the consumption recommendations for vegetables, fruits and dairy, which all provide important nutrients that support optimal health and academic success. Encouraging consumption of nutritious foods, from both plant-based and dairy sources, can help close the nutrient gaps that exist among people of all ages.
